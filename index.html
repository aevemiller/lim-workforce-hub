Human Skills Why Human Skills Rise as AI Rises
Contrary to early predictions, AI is increasing the value of human-centered skills. Research conducted by Indeed (via WEF) analyzed nearly 2,900 granular work skills—only 12.7% of tasks linked to human-centric skills showed potential for AI transformation. This means empathy, resilience, leadership, and collaboration are durably human and will become more valuable as AI handles routine cognitive work.

What AI Cannot Replace

  • Empathy & interpersonal dynamics
  • Contextual judgment from lived experience
  • Leadership under ambiguity
  • Creative synthesis across domains
  • Trust-building in human relationships
  • Resilience through novel setbacks

What Employers Cite as Barriers to AI Adoption

  • 50% — Lack of skills to support AI adoption
  • 43% — Lack of vision among managers & leaders
  • 29% — High cost of AI products
  • 24% — Lack of customization to business needs
  • Result: Human leadership & judgment remain the critical bottleneck

Policy Context

Where Leader in Me fits the policy landscape

For grants and RFPs tied to state or federal education priorities. This section maps Leader in Me to the dominant policy frameworks and explains why the policy landscape creates a specific opening for LiM.

CTE Career & Technical Education Landscape

How Employability Shows Up in State CTE

  • 42 states + DC supported development of the Common Career Technical Core (CCTC), which includes Career Ready Practices
  • 30 states espouse some vision of skills students should have at graduation
  • 20 states have formal Portrait of a Graduate frameworks
  • Most common competencies in state portraits: critical thinking, communication, self-management
  • Only 4 states require all students to take ACT WorkKeys—most assessment is voluntary

The Implementation Gap

  • Many states include employability skills in CTE frameworks—far fewer build them into statewide requirements
  • Assessment/accountability for employability skills is structurally weaker than for academic skills
  • "What is difficult to measure at scale is harder to prioritize"
  • Portraits are often strongest at naming what matters and weakest at changing classroom practice
  • LiM addresses exactly this gap: it operationalizes the Portrait through curriculum + coaching

Grant Angles Policy Hooks for Funders

Perkins V & Federal CTE Alignment

  • Perkins V requires that CTE programs prepare students for "high-skill, high-wage, or in-demand" occupations
  • Leadership and collaboration are explicitly cited as career-ready practices
  • LiM secondary curriculum directly develops career-ready practice competencies
  • Micro-credentials provide Perkins-aligned documentation of student outcomes

ESSA / ESEA / Title IV Angles

  • Title IV (Student Support and Academic Enrichment) funds programs that promote well-rounded educational opportunities
  • LiM's well-being, SEL, and leadership components align to well-rounded education priorities
  • School climate improvement is a documented LiM outcome (d = 0.34)
  • Engagement data supports ESSA's broader definition of school success
The key argument for policy-aligned funders: State Portrait of a Graduate frameworks create shared awareness. Leader in Me changes what happens in classrooms. The difference is between a vision statement and a system. Funders who want implementation—not just aspiration—should fund Leader in Me.
